Stories of Success

Turning a neurodivergent brain into a superpower

In the past, neurodivergence likely existed under different names or was misunderstood, as its modern conceptualization only emerged in the 20th century.

Many historical figures, artists, leaders, and inventors who displayed signs of neurodivergence might have been unknowingly living with one, two, or more conditions or superpowers — however you want to name it. Without formal recognition, these traits were often seen as eccentricities, genius, or even disruptive behaviors.

Today, we could use an inclusive definition such as "non-linear thinking" or "non-linear thinkers" to define that their brain worked differently than the norm.

Famous non-linear thinkers

1. Actors & actresses

Ryan GoslingHe won not only hearts but also a Golden Globe. Ryan Gosling is another example of someone who struggled in school years because of his ADHD, but had the strength to turn it into an award-winning career in Hollywood.
Emma WatsonShe is best known for her Hermione Granger role in “Harry Potter.” She was diagnosed with ADHD when she was a kid. Many sources, including ADDitude Magazine, have reported on her experience with ADHD.
Woody HarrelsonAnother award-winning actor often mentioned alongside famous people with ADHD. His symptoms didn't stop his passion for becoming a great actor.
Michelle RodriguezShe has been open about her ADHD, sharing her story in an interview with Cosmopolitan Magazine. She didn't let it hold back her career, taking on major roles in Lost and the Fast & Furious franchise.
Zooey DeschanelZooey Deschanel has demonstrated that living with ADHD may not be that difficult, proudly channeling her focus into craft projects and creative work.
Will SmithNot only an award-winning actor, but also a producer and rapper, Will Smith has spoken about having ADHD. Forbes ranked him among the “most bankable stars” in Hollywood.
Jim CarreyAward-winning actor Jim Carrey has spoken about his experience with ADHD, channeling his energy into the characters he plays.

2. Athletes

Michael PhelpsThe most decorated Olympian of all time, Phelps was diagnosed with ADHD as a child and has shared how swimming helped him manage his symptoms.
Karina SmirnoffKarina is a great example of athletes who have ADHD and who always strive for more. She has a variety of interests, from dancing and ice skating to piano and gymnastics, and has become a five-time champion.
Shaun WhiteThe snowboarding and skateboarding champion has openly discussed his ADHD diagnosis and how sports became a channel for his energy and focus.
Nicolas AnelkaThe former French footballer, who played for clubs like Real Madrid and Chelsea, has been reported to have ADHD, which has been linked to his sometimes impulsive and misunderstood on-field behavior.
Kelly HolmesThe British Olympic middle-distance runner has been reported to have ADHD, and has said her challenges with attention and focus motivated her to push harder in her career.
Terry BradshawFocusing and standing still can be challenging for people with ADHD. Terry experienced difficulties in school and shifted his focus to sports — eventually becoming a Super Bowl Champion.

SiaSia has been open about mental health challenges throughout her career, including ADHD, which she's said has shaped her creative output.
Jean Jacques GoldmanThere's limited public confirmation of an ADHD diagnosis, but the French singer-songwriter is often discussed in this context due to his bursts of creativity and the challenges of balancing intense focus with the demands of a music career.
Benjamin BiolayAnother speculative case — some fans and critics have suggested his intense creativity and impulsive career decisions reflect traits associated with ADHD, though no formal diagnosis has been confirmed.

4. Historical figures

Many historical men and women, while never formally diagnosed, exhibited behaviors that align with modern understandings of non-linear thinkers. They were not just survivors of their neurodivergent challenges—they transformed them into strengths, breaking boundaries in their fields. These traits, which today would be recognized as part of the neurodivergent spectrum, may have been a source of both their struggles and their incredible accomplishments.

Albert EinsteinEinstein's difficulties in traditional schooling — often described as forgetful and disorganized — combined with his intense hyperfocus on theoretical physics, are traits some have associated with ADHD.
Wolfgang Amadeus MozartMozart's impulsivity and boundless creativity, along with his ability to compose with extraordinary speed and intensity, have led some to suggest traits consistent with ADHD.
Walt DisneyWalt Disney is often mentioned among historical figures associated with ADHD, largely due to the difficulties he reportedly experienced during his school years.
Salvador DalíDalí's eccentric, impulsive behavior and constant need for stimulation and creative exploration are traits sometimes associated with ADHD.
Pablo PicassoPicasso's intense creative outbursts, rapid shifts between projects, and difficulty sustaining long-term focus are sometimes cited as ADHD-like tendencies.
Hans Christian AndersenThe Danish author of The Little Mermaid and other fairy tales is sometimes described as showing hyperactivity, a wandering mind, and restlessness — along with difficulty focusing on schoolwork — traits some associate with ADHD.
Alexander Graham BellThe inventor of the telephone has been described as easily distracted and impatient, with a constant drive to invent and explore new ideas despite struggles with formal schooling — traits some associate with ADHD.

5. Women

Women may struggle more with a non-linear brain due to differences in symptom manifestation and societal expectations. Neurodivergence in women often presents with more internalized symptoms, because they may face greater pressure to conform to social norms, which can exacerbate neurodivergent symptoms and hinder effective coping strategies. Despite these challenges, many women have harnessed their neurodivergent traits, such as creativity, hyperfocus, out-of-the-box thinking, giftedness, and resilience, to achieve remarkable successes and make significant contributions in various fields.

Simone de BeauvoirDe Beauvoir's prolific writing and philosophical work reflect traits some biographers associate with ADHD, including hyperfocus and a non-linear thought process, alongside a formal education marked by high achievement despite friction with traditional academic structures.
Agatha ChristieSome biographers have suggested Christie's reported difficulties with focus and organization, and a writing process marked by intense bursts of concentration, are consistent with ADHD. She was educated at home by governesses and later found her creative outlet in writing.
Coco ChanelChanel's restlessness and tendency to juggle multiple projects at once has led some to associate her creative style with ADHD traits. Her early education was sparse and unconventional, though she excelled in practical and creative skills.
Marie CurieCurie's intense focus on scientific research and ability to immerse herself completely in complex work is sometimes cited as consistent with ADHD traits, alongside a documented tendency toward impulsivity.
Frida KahloKahlo's creative, impulsive nature and bursts of intense focus have led some to suggest possible ADHD traits. Her schooling was affected by frequent absences due to health issues, shaping her distinctive path into art.
Emily DickinsonDickinson's intense focus on her writing and tendency to withdraw from social interaction have led some biographers to suggest ADHD traits. Her education was limited and irregular, and her unconventional approach shaped a singular body of work.
Dorothy ParkerSome biographers have pointed to traits consistent with ADHD in Parker's impulsivity and quick, non-linear wit, alongside a schooling experience marked by difficulty with attention that pushed her toward more informal, creative settings.
Zelda FitzgeraldZelda Fitzgerald's tendency to start multiple creative projects without finishing them, and her bursts of energy, are sometimes cited as consistent with ADHD traits — alongside a tumultuous, unconventional schooling experience.

Business & entrepreneurial leaders

Sixteen founders and executives whose non-linear minds shaped how they struggled — and how they built.

Sir Richard Branson

Founder, Virgin Group (400+ companies)

Neurodivergence
Diagnosed with ADHD and severe dyslexia, he processes information visually rather than through dense linear text.
The struggle
Flunked traditional schooling and struggled immensely with reading, financial spreadsheets, and formal corporate structure.
The success
Transformed his executive friction into a delegation strategy, trusting operational leaders to handle details while he built a global multibillion-dollar empire around big-picture strategy and brand charisma.

Jo Malone CBE

Founder, Jo Malone London & Jo Loves

Neurodivergence
Severe dyslexia that shaped her visual-spatial and sensory processing.
The struggle
Left school at age 13 without formal qualifications due to profound difficulties with traditional reading and writing.
The success
Channelled her non-linear sensory processing into an extraordinary acute sense of smell and visual product design, scaling two globally acclaimed luxury fragrance brands.

Elon Musk

Founder & CEO, Tesla, SpaceX, xAI

Neurodivergence
Openly autistic (Asperger's) paired with a high-intellectual profile.
The struggle
Experienced severe social isolation, intense sensory processing dynamics, and difficulty navigating standard interpersonal corporate norms in his early career.
The success
Harnesses relentless hyperfocus and first-principles systemic thinking to dissect complex physics and engineering challenges, disrupting automotive and aerospace industries simultaneously.

Barbara Corcoran

Founder, The Corcoran Group & Shark Tank Investor

Neurodivergence
High-energy ADHD and dyslexia.
The struggle
Labeled "stupid" in school, held 20 different jobs by age 23, and routinely battled severe literacy and organizational constraints.
The success
Built extreme emotional resilience and sharp intuitive judgment, leveraging hyper-creative marketing to turn a $1,000 loan into a $66 million real estate empire.

Ingvar Kamprad

Founder, IKEA

Neurodivergence
ADHD and dyslexia.
The struggle
Constantly lost track of numerical inventory lists, stock codes, and traditional administrative paperwork.
The success
Designed IKEA's iconic Swedish product-naming system specifically as an executive function workaround, inadvertently creating a global brand asset that simplified logistics worldwide.
